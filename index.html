Part 1

The 7 Secrets

Click each card to learn the secret — then mark it done!

🎯

One Big Idea

The secret of focus

▼
A TED Talk is NOT a report. It has ONE central idea — one message you want the audience to remember forever. Everything you say must support that one idea.
  • 1
    Write your one idea in ONE sentence.
  • 2
    Check every part of your talk: does it support this idea? If not, cut it.
  • 3
    Say your idea clearly at least once during your talk.
"Technology is changing the way we communicate — and that's actually making us lonelier."
🪝

The Hook

Grab them in 60 seconds

▼
You have 60 seconds to make the audience want to listen. Never start with "Good morning, my name is..." — that wastes your most powerful moment.
  • 1
    Choose a hook type: story, shocking fact, question, or bold statement.
  • 2
    Write your opening 3–5 sentences. Make them dramatic!
  • 3
    Practise your opening until it sounds totally natural.
"Three years ago, I nearly died. And in that moment, I understood something that changed my life completely."
📖

Tell a Story

Facts tell, stories sell

▼
Data is forgettable. Stories are unforgettable. TED speakers use personal stories, real examples, and vivid scenes to make their ideas stick in the audience's brain.
  • 1
    Find a personal story that connects to your big idea.
  • 2
    Use this pattern: Problem → Struggle → Solution/Lesson
  • 3
    Add details: describe what you saw, heard, or felt. Make it real.
"I remember standing in the hospital corridor, the fluorescent lights humming above me, thinking..."
🗣️

Your Voice

Pace, pause & power

▼
Your voice is your most powerful tool. Most students speak too fast when nervous. TED speakers slow down, vary their speed, and use silence as a weapon.
  • 1
    Slow down for important ideas. Speed up for exciting parts.
  • 2
    Pause for 2–3 seconds after your most important sentence. Let it land.
  • 3
    Record yourself and listen back. Are you monotone? Vary your tone.
"And then — [pause] — everything changed."
🚶

Own the Stage

Body language = confidence

▼
55% of your message comes from your body language. TED speakers stand open, move with purpose, and use eye contact to connect with every person in the room.
  • 1
    Stand open: no crossing arms, no hiding behind a podium.
  • 2
    Move with purpose: step forward to make a key point, step back to pause.
  • 3
    Eye contact: look at one person for 2–3 seconds, then move to another.
Hold eye contact when you say your most important line — don't look at your notes.
🔁

Rule of Three

Make it memorable

▼
Humans naturally remember things in groups of three. TED speakers always cluster their points in threes. It sounds more professional, more confident, and is easier to remember.
  • 1
    Organise your talk into 3 main points — not 2, not 5.
  • 2
    Signal it clearly: "First... Second... And finally..."
  • 3
    Use three-word phrases for extra impact.
"It is fast, it is free, and it is changing the world."
💥

Killer Ending

End with a BANG

▼
"Thank you for listening" is a weak ending. The last words the audience hears are what they remember most. Go out with a powerful call to action or a full-circle callback to your opening.
  • 1
    Option A – Call to Action: tell the audience exactly what you want them to do.
  • 2
    Option B – Full Circle: return to your opening story and show how it ends.
  • 3
    Memorise your last two sentences. Never look at your notes for the ending.
"So the next time you pick up your phone, ask yourself: am I connecting — or am I hiding?"

📖 Personal Story Hook

Start with a real moment from your life. Transport the audience to that scene. Make them feel they are there with you. A story instantly builds emotional connection and trust.

"I was 16 years old, sitting in the back of my father's car, when he said something that completely changed how I see the world..."
